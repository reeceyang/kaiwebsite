la la land

August 08, 2024

Everyone in LA has the same music taste as me. That isn’t true at all but sometimes that’s how it feels. Everyone’s obsessed with the new Clairo. Everyone’s holding on to the old Peach Pit. Everyone’s crooning to Weyes Blood. Every cafe I enter has half my playlist on loop. Call me basic, but it makes sense—it’s these cafes that shaped my music taste in the first place.

I return to southern California every break to see my family, but since my mom moved out of Los Angeles during COVID I haven’t really been back here, able to wring out my days in the sun on these mile-wide sidewalks, weaving through fumes of taco meat and piss and spray paint and gasoline. Someone’s playing jazz down the street. Someone else’s cursing me out. Meanwhile sculpted granite reaches into the sky.

LA is mostly a wide city—it doesn’t have the same vertical sprawl that NYC does—so when you’re up high you really feel it: looking out the window of the twelfth story of my grandparents’ retirement home with every dazzling, building-tall mural in a mile radius visible to you. It feels wide even when you’re in it. There’s something horizontal about the way the sleekness of the Apple Store squeezes between the thrumming 90s neon of the Orpheum Theater and the flashy baubles of La Bonita Jewelry.

My excuse for coming back is to have a few days’ vacation with my partner Reece, who is interning in San Jose this summer. I brought her to my old house in East LA, inhabited now by a different family that didn’t bother us as we stood in front and reminisced creepily.

The house is this salmon-colored thing built on a hill with the back deeper than the front, nestled behind a fence that I climbed over, once ripping my shorts, every time I got home later or left home earlier than when our landlord unlocked the chain on the front gate. If you leave to the left then you meet figs squashing underfoot, simmering sweetly in the heat. If you leave to the right then you meet the neighbor’s tortoise that I greeted every day on my walk to school. I showed Reece both paths. The tortoise wasn’t there anymore, but the figs were.

We went to a bookstore downtown and I picked up Kerouac’s On the Road. I had tried reading it before, if you count flipping to a random page to see if it was interesting and getting bored within a paragraph “trying”. That day, for some reason, I felt compelled to start from the beginning. On this read, it ticked all the boxes for me—while I’m reading I can pretend I’m an adventurer, too, hitchhiking across America, meeting new people, huddling with just-met friends beneath tarpaulin sheets on the back of a truck to keep warm. 

Later we went to Doheny State Beach and rented a wetsuit and surfboard each and paddled out into the water. I kept staring at this guy in front of me who’d catch wave after wave and then glide back out, damp golden curls resting on a broad back as red with sunburn as the sky. Every so often pelicans flew overhead, their heads and bodies so bulky and held so awkwardly in comparison to their wings that it seemed certain they would plummet at any moment. Somewhere behind me a surfer was telling his friend about DDT: “The eggshells got way too thin, dude. They had to stop putting that stuff in pesticide.” It was my first time surfing. When I saw a dark crest in the distance I would turn towards shore and start paddling furiously, holding my breath for what felt like minutes, wondering when the wave would hit, and then I’d be lifted—suddenly, quietly, inexplicably—bulk and awkwardness and all—into the sky.

While Reece and I walked back to the rental shop to return our surfboards, I felt it, the silent opening of a cold pit in my stomach. I realized after some poking around inside that it was the feeling of my own mortality. As dramatic as it sounds, it’s the only explanation; maybe you, too, have felt the weight of an inevitable ending that makes you feel dark and empty and un-fill-able like nothing else.

Maybe the feeling was because this is where I grew up, one foot in the Rockies and the other in the Pacific, and nostalgia is twined together with loss. Or maybe it was because my impression of becoming an East Coast academic, just the opposite of all my childhood was seeped in, came with a coldness and lifelessness brimming, too, with immortality. There are no pits opening inside me in Boston: no hole there is too big to fill. As long as you wrangle your dream into a ladder to climb—at a startup, in a fintech firm, or in academia—then if you work hard enough in New England you can do it.

The narrator of On the Road is this East Coast boy who decides to go on a road trip with his best friend to Denver and then San Francisco. Maybe every time I tried reading it before, I didn’t understand his hunger for the West. But the more time I spend on the East Coast myself, the more I understand how improbable the West is.

The East Coast is where I would go if I knew roughly what I wanted to accomplish and how to do it. When a well-placed ladder exists, climbing it is very useful. But Los Angeles is different, and this I’d forgotten after spending a few years in college feeling immortal. LA is a city for the idealistic, those who would rather do the unnecessary work of building a new system to fit their dream than to bend their dream into any existing system. City of dreamers, city of angels, city of stars—in the end you stand in the city of the improbable.
