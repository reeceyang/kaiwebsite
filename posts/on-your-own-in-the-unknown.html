on your own (in the unknown)

July 30, 2023

Maybe I should’ve predicted this, based on how summer research generally goes, but it surprised me anyway: the research I’m doing has been the least memorable part of this summer. Instead I’ve fallen unexpectedly in love with the place, the art, the people—and the feeling of being on my own, which I’ve missed for so long.

A week ago, I swam across a reservoir without stopping, the longest continuous distance I’ve swum ever, and loved it; I climb and hike and I lift weights in the gym—not heavy ones yet, but they’re getting heavier. And I have come to really enjoy both the process and the progress. The feeling of my muscles becoming taut and round over time, growing where I never thought I would, watching my body become stronger and sturdier. I feel so much more capable now that I have some strength, and now that I know I will continue building it.

I have tried to go to the gym before this summer, often with other people. But being with others only compounded my anxiety about being in the gym, the threat of being watched, the fear of doing something wrong, the inferiority compared to other people who are curling 60s. Words of encouragement felt like condescension: it didn’t matter if someone told me “you can do it!” or “maybe try a lighter weight”—it felt the same, like we all knew deep down I would never succeed.

What finally kept me in the gym was the independence. I woke up at six in the morning, ate breakfast and got ready in half an hour, then walked to the gym—a half-hour walk bathed in sunrise light, dawn birdsong, ochre hues lining Santa Fe’s main road—and worked out for an hour before getting on the bus to work. I didn’t have to wait for anyone to pick a routine or critique my form. It was just me picking up weights and putting them back down—me challenging myself, not a comparison to the swole rando the next machine down, not my friends being polite. And I was the only person keeping myself accountable.

(As an addendum, a big part of the reason I had enough confidence to go to the gym and work out by myself was because I watched so many gym videos on Instagram. I’ve probably wasted a whole month of my life on those things, but they are really educational if you want to know what elements of form to focus on and how to build a routine for a certain fitness goal.)

Being able to use my body to do some things gives me confidence to use it to do other things. What can I try to do now?—anything! I can dream of learning spins and jumps on the ice. I can plan out long-distance swims. I have generally disdained running for as long as I’ve known people doing it recreationally, but recently, I ran by myself (by choice!) for the first time, weaving through the Santa Fe foliage, on a dark asphalt path between short leafy trees. Nothing but me and the air, damp from rain, and the birds. In the past I became out of breath, unable to go on, after a couple of miles. This time I ran eight miles. The path opened up into tranquil meadows and sparse suburbs, and I marveled at how much of Santa Fe’s beauty I had never seen before.

To be transparent, I was also semi-rejected recently (it’s complicated), for the first time in my life. We were cool about it and we’re still friends. But being forced to deal with this situation has forced me to reckon with being alone. I used to be good at being alone—in high school, I did most everything on my own. I hopped on buses to see shitty movies by myself: Mission Impossible and Ocean’s 8 and whatever was showing at Regal in downtown LA. I walked all over LA, through Little Tokyo, through Chinatown, miles and miles of green parks and yellow neighborhoods.

Then came college, and suddenly I was surrounded by people all the time. Every moment I didn’t spend with others was a moment I spent worrying about wasting my college years away. So after two years of psetting with friends, eating with friends, window shopping with friends, I’m better at carrying on a conversation, but I’m worse at being by myself. I no longer retreat into my mind and stay there for hours, forgetting other people.

But now that, post-rejection, I no longer feel like I can hang out as often with the person I am closest to here, I’m forced to do more things on my own. And Santa Fe is a lovely place for that. The desert demands that you exist in solitude. Out at a cafe, I am surrounded by other artists and writers filling their notebooks. The sun beats down on my walks, and there is no reprieve: no coolness offered by the undergrowth that gluts other cities; no flowing waters to dust one’s skin with a spray of moisture; no AC-filled, cold and crisp chain supermarkets. Just your mind in your body in your skin, under the sun, between the mountains, reaching for possibilities you don’t yet know.
