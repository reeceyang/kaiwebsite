January in media

February 03, 2024

I barely ever read non-technical material or watch shows during the semester. This isn’t so much because I don’t have time to, say, read a chapter a day of some fiction book, but because it’s really hard for me to stop once I start and suddenly eight hours have passed and my psets remain undone. (I thought nonfiction would be exempt from this unfortunate recurrence, but this was proven wrong when I picked up In the Dream House this fall, which was phenomenal and also probably singlehandedly lowered my 8.09 grade by half a letter.) But, unfettered by weekly psets, I went ham on media consumption this month and wanted to share some of the things I liked!

I’ll be talking about Bomb Power by Garry Wills; The Remains of the Day by Kazuo Ishiguro; and Yuri!!! on Ice (2016), whose movie is as good as cancelled but which will forever remain famous in my heart. (I also read Nuclear Folly by Serhii Plokhy, which was about the Cuban missile crisis, but I don’t really have anything to say about it. I’m glad we all avoided nuclear Armageddon?)

Yuri!!! on Ice

This is perhaps best known as “the gay ice skating anime” and I’m always a little embarrassed to tell people it’s actually my favorite anime (to be fair, I’ve watched fewer than ten shows in full, ever). But I have always related a lot to Yuri, the main character, albeit in different ways throughout my life; and as I’ve grown, I’ve developed a greater appreciation for the subtle ways in which the characters’ relationship is shown.

I first watched this show the year it came out, in 2016. I was in eighth grade. At the time I enjoyed it but wanted to see more explicit communication between the two main characters—like, why weren’t they actually telling each other I love you if their relationship was ostensibly romantic?

The second time I watched this show was junior year of high school: late 2019 or early 2020, I can’t remember (I know it was pre-COVID). By then, I had entered my first romantic relationship and understood more how subtle gestures could communicate love, and found the show actually a bit overwhelming in that aspect. But at that point, I also understood my issues with anxiety more as a fundamentally crippling aspect of myself and not something I could overcome; so Yuri’s anxiety, and the ways in which he seems to just brush it away at some points in the show, failed to ring true to me.

The third time around was this January. I watched two episodes a night with my partner, who was seeing it for the first time. In some ways, the objective flaws of the series were more apparent to me: the animators obviously took great displeasure in actually animating all the ice skating scenes, so they would cut away often to flashbacks the characters were having, or to reused clips of audience reactions. The structure of the show is also just going through a bunch of skating programs from irrelevant people, one after the other, with very little time in between to explore character dynamics and development (which I think most people would agree is the most appealing part of the show). Also, the animation really sucks in some places. I think this show won a Crunchyroll award for best animation quality when it came out, which is really funny.

But this time when I watched it, all the themes felt true and correct to me in how they interfaced with my own life. In the show, Yuri treats his own anxiety as a “mental weakness” that he must overcome to do his best. For me as well, thinking of my anxiety and mental issues in general less as defining characteristics and more as temporary weaknesses has helped me become more capable. To be clear, I don’t think my diagnoses have necessarily been wrong, but thinking of their symptoms as weaknesses to work on has been more helpful to me than thinking of them as immovable, genetically preordained aspects of my being, curable only with a daily 40mg of Citalopram.

Then the relationships and character development of the main three are lovely, of course. Also it was cool to watch ice skating now that I actually know how to ice skate!!

The Remains of the Day

I don’t even know what to say about this book. It’s about an English butler who takes a road trip and thinks about his life. It was so legitimately heartbreaking—a descriptor I can apply to a maximum of maybe four books I’ve ever read. I think someone called Adina on Goodreads put it well:

Although this quote [from the book] depicts the magnificent English countryside, it can be applied to the novel as well:

“What is pertinent is the calmness of beauty, its sense of restraint. It is as though the land knows of its own beauty, its own greatness, and feels no need to shout it.”

I believe that a restrained beauty is what characterizes The Remains of the Day and the voice of its main character, Stevens. As was also the case in Never Let Me Go, the message is hidden in the beautiful pages, only suggested, it comes to the reader in the form of a knot in the stomach or throat and the feelings linger for many days while one ponders on the meaning of his/her life.

This is basically how I feel about all of Ishiguro’s writing: it’s impressively restrained, yet always conveys some really deep sense of emotion. It’s also very fluid to read and you’re always immersed entirely into the narrator’s mind. I can’t recommend the book enough.

Bomb Power

I read this for a book group that my lab is holding in a couple of weeks. It traces the tightening security apparatus around the U.S. government from the Manhattan Project to the early 2010s, when the book was published. Basically, Wills makes the argument that the advent of the atomic bomb, as well as all the infrastructure and procedures put in place to keep the Manhattan Project a secret even from Congress, made it possible—and set a precedent—to justify executive overreach on all manner of other aspects of government.

For example: the Manhattan Project led to the creation of the CIA and NSA, which were initially supposed to be “information-gathering” agencies only, and we all know how that turned out. This book revealed to me some of the actual mechanisms behind generic “U.S. government do bad thing” stories you hear about, like torture and covert operations against foreign governments and civilian surveillance; many of them are results of executive overreach, or as Wills puts it, a snowballing of legislative power into the hands of the executive branch (the President), who was never meant to wield it.

Wills notes that only Congress can declare war or make legislation, yet the President has routinely bypassed Congress to achieve both these goals in recent decades while justifying his decisions with access to classified information or the urgency of a crisis that may require use of nuclear weapons. In many passages, Wills’s implication is that Congress would make a more reasoned decision that better reflected the views of the American populace. For instance, John McCain’s bill against state-sanctioned torture passed stunningly in both the Senate and House and then was maimed by Bush in a signing statement which basically meant the CIA could continue to ignore Congressional directives.

Yet perhaps, in general, a President unbeholden to “insider connections” and the whims of lobbyists actually better reflects the will of the people—this is what Trump supporters believe about him. I felt that this viewpoint wasn’t adequately addressed; sure, it’s unconstitutional, but there’s a reason expanding executive power gets so much support. Perhaps this all started with the bomb, but a large factor that keeps this power in the hands of the President alone today seems to be the people’s distrust of Congress. Until that’s addressed, I think none of the rest will change.
