ode to my ankle

April 06, 2024

Four Saturdays ago1, I slipped and fell on a bouldering wall—a slab, or one of the walls that slope inwards, so that you fall into the wall instead of away from it—and my foot hit a bunch of holds on the way down. I curled up and moaned for a while and then got up and found that I could not put any weight on my left ankle.

This was disappointing, but I had experienced mildly debilitating foot pains in the past and figured it would be fine soon. I Ubered back home and crawled around for the rest of the day. It was hard to work or sleep; I thought I would go to Medical the next day and get an x-ray.

Sunday morning, I dragged myself to Medical on crutches, taking nearly half an hour to traverse the distance between Lobby 7 and Ames Street. Usually a pleasant stroll, now the journey rendered me dehydrated and deeply sad by the time I reached my destination. Woe betide me!—despite the consensus that I really needed an x-ray by the three medical professionals who looked at my foot, Medical did not offer them on weekends. I retreated to the Banana Lounge in defeat and Ubered home.

By the end of that day, my foot had swollen into an unrecognizable shape—like, not even foot-shaped anymore, convex in all the places it should have been concave—and was bruised purple. My armpits and wrists were both sore from using the crutches. I still could not properly work or sleep: the mere existence of meat attached to my ankle made the joint hurt, despite continuously elevating and icing it. Thank God for my girlfriend who got me food and kept me functional; even with her help, I felt morally defeated by the whole situation: not being able to retrieve the smallest of items by myself, having to scoot up and down three flights of stairs on my ass to access my room, changing my clothes becoming a many-step ordeal, somehow also losing my toothbrush in the midst of all this??2

Anyway, on Monday I did get the x-ray, and it turned out my foot was not fractured. Once I received this news I felt reassured: I would hopefully not have to deal with this for much longer.3 After a few days I could put some weight on my foot, which made using crutches much easier. The people at Medical also gave me a really OP ankle brace that made my foot feel much better when it was in a resting position.

It feels obvious but somehow surprises me every time I say it: everything is so physical, so tied to the body. Nowadays much of my joy comes from physical things. Building circuits and twiddling knobs with my hands, watching real physics happen. Lifting weights, as slow as my progress is. Just walking around. I felt the lack of these things as a deep, hollow ache when my ankle was out of commission.

That Monday after getting the x-ray, I went with my girlfriend to this on-campus music studio that she facilitates events in. With the power of a ChatGPT-written budget proposal, she had secured funds for an electric guitar, electric bass, and electric drum kit for the studio.

Once the instruments were set up and the other people there had left, she took the drums and I tried out the bass. We played an Inner Wave song on repeat, trying to follow along to the drum and bass lines. I hadn’t had practice identifying the bass line under all the other instruments before: it sat near the back of my head, in the center, just in front of my spine. It was exhilarating when we sounded good, and still fun when we didn’t. I forgot about my temporarily shitty ankle for the first time in three days, there in the studio focusing on my fingers running over frets, nothing but our mostly functional bodies and the music.
