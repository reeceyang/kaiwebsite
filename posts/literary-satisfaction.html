poetry

I started reading Twice Alive, a poetry collection by Forrest Gander. I got this book at a sale in Kendall Square in the fall. I was always one of those people who didn’t “get” poetry, but finally, recently, I’ve found some appeal in the genre.

When you can feel the buildup to the final line—something carnal intertwined with a higher mode of thought, a cortex-controlled resistance to the climax you know in your soul is coming, like your head spinning before you throw up, your mouth watering before the first bite of food, holding on to the final beats before the drop, the moments before orgasm—most poems don’t do this for me, but the ones that do are fucking insane, genre- and life-defining.

Here’s one, not by Forrest Gander; here’s another (I like the first one on the page, called Son), from a different Forrest Gander collection; here’s a third, from Twice Alive, the last stanza of which is reproduced here.

By now, some of us, outmaneuvered by the economy,
were lying around Dolores Park like fallen fruit
waiting to rot. Others found themselves receptive
to a trivial, self-justifying kindness.
What with coral belching up its algae, evaporating stars,
the waking tundra, how could we bear, we wondered to
each other, even the weight of
our own sorry initiatives? Life, someone
countered, is pure gratuitous magnitude. Just
look: the light is there, grace itself. But
it was already noon and as we looked,
the colors of the hills began to blanch,
and all around us, in the field of the visible, we
sensed, without speaking, duration’s ebb.

Doesn’t the last line hit like a brick? Whenever I read this poem, I have to put my hand under the line I’m on so that I don’t skip ahead. The anticipation makes me physically start shaking and salivating. That’s really gross, but my point is it’s kind of insane that words can do that to a person.

Why doesn’t more poetry feel like this? I guess poetry is incredibly dense—every word matters, makes some first-order alteration to the trajectory of the piece—so maybe it takes a very close reading to achieve this feeling with most poems, a level of attention I haven’t breached. But there’s also merit to poetry that doesn’t demand such a level of attention to still punch you in the gut.

Here’s a post I enjoyed recently. People on Substack are really obsessed with writing anti-digital-era pieces, which gets grating after a while (even though I almost always agree with the thesis) because not all of them are very good at it, but this piece is both well-written and legitimately delivers new ways to think about the psychological role phones play in our lives.

Not using a phone taught me what a phone is really for. It’s not for communicating with other people, getting directions, reading articles, looking at pictures, shopping for products, or playing games. A phone is a device for muting the anxieties proper to being alive. This is what all its functions and features ultimately achieve: cameras deliver you from time, GPS abstracts you out of space, and an all-consuming screen that keeps you a constant safe distance from yourself. If there’s something you’re worried or upset about, you can simply hide behind your phone and it will all go away. One third of adults say they’re on their phones almost constantly. Their entire waking lives are spent filling time, plastering over the gaps, burning up one day after another, waiting for something to happen, and it never does.

As for my own writing, for the first time I’m in the position of having a backlog of topics to write about. I have a lot of thoughts about these topics and want to do them justice, but I don’t have the time to actually research and write and revise at the moment. I’m missing last summer in Santa Fe, when I could take a whole weekend to write a post by hand, in the shade outside a cafe where other writers and artists sat hunched over their notebooks too. Now, there’s always something else to do.
