acadia & adulthood

June 22, 2022

It’s hard to decide what to value in the future. I went on a camping trip to Acadia National Park in Maine this past weekend with the MIT Outing Club (MITOC), and I marveled continually at the landscape throughout – jagged ocean rocks, misty horizons, vivid foliage. I love the mountains and the ocean, and Maine has both, for cheap. But I also want to live somewhere walkable, somewhere with lots of people, somewhere I can do my best science. This isn’t something I can make any decisions about right now, though.

MITOC is quite dominated by older adults. By “older” I mean grad students, working adults, and the occasional professor. Meeting and talking with these people has been one of my favorite parts of MITOC trips. It makes me feel more at ease about my future. During car rides and hikes, adults interact with each other, and with me, in much the same way as I interact with my current friends.

Although this might seem obvious, it made me feel relieved to see that people don’t have to lose their sense of humor or childishness as they grow older. And so many of these people were still learning and growing, not settling for the stagnation that I had feared: I met folks biking across the US, planning their first backpacking trips, ending careers and starting them, too. Always, adults I talked to were uncertain of their futures, just as I am.

On Saturday, we went kayaking and hiking. These both were very fun: on a tandem kayak, my girlfriend and I swerved from side to side the entire time, unable to figure out how to stay on a straight course until the last few minutes. We hiked up Beech Mountain afterwards. It was not a strenuous hike, which was a nice change of pace from usual MITOC outings. I still have deep purple bruises from my hike up Mt. Eisenhower nearly two weeks ago; in contrast, I was able to just take in the beauty of our surroundings while walking up Beech at a leisurely pace.

On Sunday, we joined a group of cyclists to ride the Carriage Roads, an expansive network of walk- and bike-only paths looping around the ponds on the island. I had not been on a bike in years and was mildly nervous, but I didn’t want to leave Acadia without at least cycling on an easy route.

In hindsight, I probably should have been a lot more nervous than I was. There were so many points along the ride at which I thought, I am literally going to die, I can’t do this, my thighs physically can’t go any more, but I didn’t die. The others in our group taught me how to shift gears and were patient with me when I fell behind; they checked with me to make sure I was okay when we took breaks. The first time I switched to the lowest gear, the guy biking next to me nodded: “That’s how you climb mountains.”

The ride ended up being upwards of 15 miles. It was beautiful; the rain came down, mostly soft but sometimes piercing and crashing, when I would focus on the drops soaking into my pant legs to take my mind off the pain. It wasn’t a matter of pushing myself to pedal harder so much as being honest and patient, accepting the constant ache in my legs, knowing that if I kept going then I would make it – and deciding to keep going.

Most things these days are just a matter of deciding to keep going. I think that is the hardest thing. Deciding to take on a tour guide shift even when I am anxious about it; deciding to work for another hour even when I am bored and tired; hardest of all, deciding not to melt into my guilt and sadness and, instead, to confront and push myself every day.

By the end of the ride I switched gears intuitively, assessing the grade in front of me and clicking in with my right hand. We made it back onto the streets of Bar Harbor, stopped for a bit to walk our bikes across the interstate, then hopped back on the bikes and kept going. I was nervous, here: I am not very good at seamlessly mounting the bike and riding without a hitch, which is quite important on busy streets. I wobbled a bit, getting on. And then it was fine.
