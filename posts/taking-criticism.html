taking criticism

December 18, 2021

in high school I was terrible at taking criticism. I wasn’t mean about it or anything, just overly sensitive. every remark about how I might be able to improve felt like a personal attack.

this was true even when there was no person directly giving the criticism. I’d be studying physics, working my way through the textbook’s practice problems, and telling myself I would do just one more problem before checking my work for them, to procrastinate the feeling of having failed—of being wrong. in high school, to some part, this was because I felt insecure about my skill and was worried I would get more wrong one day than I had the day before—that I’d be regressing. as a result, many days I worked through problems unwilling to look at the answers at all; some problems went eternally unchecked, my stubborn self thinking he could figure them out.

I knew, going into college, that this would have to change—at the very least, that I would have to change this if I wanted to get the most out of my time at MIT. for a while I was able to avoid it: my pset scores were generally 95 or higher, and I chalked up the extra 5 to sign errors, maybe, and never looked at what my mistakes actually were; my scores on my first midterms for classes were uniformly very high as well. but then I received my grade on the second midterm for 8.03—an 82, not terrible, but certainly not optimal.

it took me some time to bring myself to look at what I actually got wrong. I had the Gradescope tab open for a whole day on my laptop, but couldn’t bear to actually look at it. finally at some point I was sitting at the table in stickman kitchen while people cooked around me, and I asked: “do you guys ever ask about exam questions during office hours?”

“definitely,” Sierra said; Bianca nodded in agreement. “they’re very helpful. you should go if you have any questions. sometimes they made mistakes grading, too, and your grade can rise by like ten points if you just go ask about it and clarify.”

this made me feel better, although I had no intention of actually going to office hours; I just wanted the courage to actually look at my exam in the first place. buoyed by the reassurance that other people also did badly on tests sometimes, I retreated to my room, took a deep breath, and clicked onto the Gradescope tab to inspect my mistakes.

and here is the thing: barring the initial wince at seeing red crosses where I was used to seeing green check marks, going over the exam made me feel so much better. many of my mistakes were silly oversights that I clarified almost immediately, which soothed my worries about fundamentally not understanding 8.03 material.

this has been a trend throughout my first semester here—that I’ll receive some feedback on something I worked on, dread the prospect of looking at it, and then actually feel better afterwards. it turns out that most people are kind in giving criticism, that they mean for it to improve your work in the end—and it really does. mostly, though, I feel happy that I have improved in this aspect since high school. it’s hard to quantify character development, and sometimes I worry I’m regressing as a person compared to my peak junior year self, but then there are little things like this that remind me that I am growing here, even if I do not feel it.
