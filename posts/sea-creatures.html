sea creatures

June 15, 2025

You can’t truly know it—but, like a beating heart, still you must sense that your world has changed. Washed up outside the body, a sonic wave breaching vacuum. The concept of movement vanished with that of time. Before, you measured the seconds against pulses of muscle; now gravity pins your tentacles together in a tangle on the sand.

At the same time this world is so sparse and still that your previous life spent being churned between unending peals of convection feels like a dream. If you had a centralized nervous system you might categorize, if only crudely and informally, one as ocean and one as land—baby’s first abstraction—and you might think of the dying beats of your bell as struggle, as preference for life. Instead all I can conceive of for you is heart gouged from body. Easily, smoothly, in one evening’s high tide, removed from the singular context you evolved for. No wonder that every little copy of you speckling the beach strikes an instinct towards repellence. Aliens invading, shouldered by hunks of brown seaweed as large and thick as several humans stitched together crawling up the bank, multicolored plastics battered but intact, all washed ashore to produce an unearthly composition.

And if I scooped you into my palms and lifted you up to look—with eyes I imagine for you—out at glittering green crests chiseled from jade, you would make no sense of this cold surface I think of with wonder as your home. Your rhopalia might perceive the elevation. Your barbed nematocysts might fill my flesh with venom. If I am to pitch you back into the sea—into turbulence, into current and foam and tide, into someday the mouth of a turtle who feels something like relief that this time it has struck true, as you nestle in its bloated intestine packed against hard alien forms of polyethylene that danced with perturbations in thermocline just as you did—I will have to draw my arm back, load the tension soon.

I considered keeping my distance, bounding over you and your translucent brethren. Or, instead, reaching inside to make the switch—one beating heart for another, an atrium you could fill like gas, piston pumping seawater a thinner bluer blood through artery from ventricle, just as I once shot veins of industrial polymer through your encasing. Fine then. Fair trade. Your chance at life seeps through my fingers, my knees hit the sand.

As a child I thought of being a “naturalist”, whatever that meant. I labeled leaf and feather samples in notebook pages, recorded footage of birds and lizards that I chopped together into documentaries. I made a website of links to factory farming exposé videos and when I showed it to my science class my best friend threw up. I was nine and she eleven. When my father asked if I could make one change to the world what would it be, I said, seriously, I would ban hunting. Hunting! He was exasperated then; I’m exasperated now, to think that once I was convinced predation was the ultimate cruelty, worse than this cycle of benthic apathy: my contamination of abyssal plain, yours of continental shelf.

I always loved the deep sea, a landscape as foreign as the surface of the Moon. You could just as easily convince me of your lunar origins, demonstrate a different kind of buoyancy. When I look through your body I see stars dotting pastel capillaries. I dreamed of understanding you, once; I thought that one lifetime’s work would be enough. Body paralyzed, watching your oral cavity flutter open and closed, if only I could tell you what I know now: you can’t comprehend why I chose you any more than I can distinguish you from another jellyfish on the beach. Your liquid discs all looked the same, protruding demurely from sand.

Do you think jellyfish go to heaven? I ask you. Flies buzz overhead. The question means nothing to you. All you have in each moment is the moment itself. If you held my hand I would die riding on the back of a photon through a memory of being loved; that’s the infinity I ask of the Earth. To you, death is a shift from one eddy to the next.
