beantown blues

August 07, 2022

I'm back in Boston now, and the sun is always out. When I'm inside, blessed by tEp's central AC, the brownstone buildings outside my window look beautiful, glittering and rustic. It's harder to appreciate them while I'm coated by sweat outside — the air feels hot and smothering even if a breeze sweeps the street or I'm passing through the shade cast by the trees and foliage.

Yesterday I walked to campus to begin building the set for the MIT Musical Theatre Guild's production of Trail to Oregon. I'm assistant carpenter and co-scenic painter for this production, and I was excited to learn more about how set design and construction worked. MTG's sets are built in a set shop in the basement of Walker Memorial; it's a space shared with other theatre groups on campus like the Shakespeare Ensemble.

There was no AC in the set shop, but it was at least marginally cooler than being roasted by the sun outside. Xinyu, our technical director, was there; she asked me to select and cut the wood needed for our build of the wagon, which is the only piece in the set of this production that's even mildly complicated to build. She has a level of quiet, well-placed confidence about nearly every aspect of theatre tech. After I cut down the wood we needed for the wagon, together we applied a base coat of paint to the flats (large, flat pieces of wood that are used as backdrops in the set). I asked her why she did tech, and the answer was surprisingly similar to mine (though maybe lots of theatre tech people have a similar motivation) — she loved musicals, loved being a part of all the songs, but wasn't practiced enough to do cast, compared to all the vocally- and musically-trained people we have in MTG.

I took some extra, unneeded wood from the set shop for my own purposes upon leaving. At night, my partner and I cut the wood down in tEp's basement wood shop into thin slabs that would form a frame for a mosquito net covering my turtle tank. (I'm adopting a turtle from a nearby rescue center soon!)

After this mosquito net frame is done, I'll continue to set up the tank. This entails covering the bottom with substrate, setting up compatible aquatic plants, arranging all the light fixtures, and so on. I kept turtles during high school and loved it, probably to an unreasonable extent, given that the turtles don't do anything for you in return except stretch out lazily on their basking docks and provide visual entertainment. I've never kept live plants in an aquarium before, though, and I'm excited for that.

My UROP continues: I read papers on plasma physics and note with amusement how often my PI's name shows up in them; I do math; I talk with my grad student. I'm not working 40 hours a week like I thought I'd be, but I'm trying to be okay with that.

I bought a book about practical electronics; I want to start fiddling with circuits again. I made overly-seasoned salt-and-pepper tofu for house dinner, which was generally well received. My partner and I have cooked tomato stew and pasta together and kept the leftovers to eat throughout the week. We also work out every morning in tEp's basement gym; it's discouraging how little I can do, but I also think it improves my energy levels and general wellbeing, and I hope that I'll make progress soon.

When I arrived back in Boston, I took the Silver Line from the airport to South Station. The signs and buildings we passed felt familiar to me in a way that I thought they shouldn't, having lived here for only one year, as a sheltered college student, no less. But I've grown attached to this town.
