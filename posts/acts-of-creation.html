acts of creation

May 29, 2023

One.

I am a college student—a physics and math major with a minor in Chinese.

Two.

“I’m going to be an animator.” I’m nine and sitting on the school bus, paused in my sketch. A half-finished character design is etched in the pages of the thick white binder on my lap.

“An animator? You’re too smart for that,” says the girl in front of me. She pops her bubblegum between her lips, chews loudly even though gum’s not allowed on the bus, which makes me twitch with anxiety. “It’d be a waste.”

I’m miffed. And surprised. Art is hard, much harder than anything they teach in school; if anything, I think you need to be smarter to do art than math or science or whatever this girl would consider not a waste.

I can’t think of a good way to convey this to her, though, so I just shrug, and she turns back to her friends. It’s not uncommon for people to interrogate me on the bus—they give me their math problems to solve, ask me for my score on the NWEA, and, as this girl just did, wonder about my career prospects.

When I get home, I log onto DeviantArt, talk with my online friends, then take out my tablet and draw until sundown.

Three.

I still want to be an animator when I’m twelve. Animator or writer—I haven’t decided. I’ve written hundreds of thousands of words of stories, probably a million if you count all my journalling, scenes and plot points I jot down obsessively in the time that remains after I rush through my tests. I enter writing contests on Wattpad, and sometimes I win them.

Writing assuages my loneliness as nothing else does. I’m still depressed, I still stare into the mirror and wonder if I’m going clinically insane, but I write and write and write and somehow it makes things make sense.

It’s the summer after eighth grade when suddenly I find that writing isn’t enough anymore, and the weight of my loneliness comes crashing down on me. It is around the same time that I become increasingly enamored with space, with biology, with the thought of putting together an airplane with my hands and watching it soar.

Six journals sit on my shelf, filled with thickened, yellowed leaves of paper; a seventh will never be touched past the first ten pages. I won’t have the time or need to write anymore, now that I have something to chase that is so big and ambitious that it makes my loneliness seem laughable.

Four.

Tenth grade. I spend my days studying physics, earth science, chemistry. It’s hard and rewarding and sustainable. My days are optimized for doing the most studying: I wake up early, a few hours before school starts, to do something light (like reviewing flashcards), nap for a bit before school, steal minutes from each class to read my textbooks, nap after school, then study until past midnight. There is not much leeway for creative pursuits.

But you cannot take the artist out of the man, so my notes for class are nearly unreadable sometimes for all the sketches spilling over the margins; I put too much effort into my English assignments, twist the prompts for an excuse at injecting prose and fantasy into a standard essay. When the pandemic hits, I have more time to draw, to write, even to compose some songs, but it’s always stolen time—always squeezed between my real work.

Five.

I’m publicity designer for Into the Woods, a musical that a club at MIT is putting on. I get roped into being their sound engineer, too. I figure it would be a productive skill to learn.

I run sound for the first rehearsal and am blown away. Orchestrating the highs and lows of the musical—hearing the actors’ voices swell, knowing I am the person allowing this to happen—feeling the story unfold, like a flower blooming larger with each song. It’s exhilarating and wild. It fills me with the joy of creation that I haven’t felt for so long.

After Into the Woods, I don’t continue with sound engineering—on top of everything else in the semester, it was too stressful. That is what I tell myself, anyway, though the stress of fitting six-hour chunks of sound testing into my bulging workweek is miniscule compared to the anxiety of doing badly in my creative endeavors.

Months after the last performance, I take the bus to New York and see Into the Woods on Broadway. It’s as wonderful as I remember, despite—or maybe because of—having seen the musical a dozen times by now, and with every song I am seized with the insatiable, intensely physical urge to wind myself between the notes of the music, to bite into them like peaches dribbling with sound and light.

One.

The more I wait, the more I worry that this liquid creative force will harden, stick to the walls inside of me until I have to scrape it out. I’m jealous of my girlfriend, who produces a song almost every month, who’s entrenched in the medium—music—that affects me most intensely. More than that, I am jealous that she is consistently creating.

I could be, too. I have more freedom with respect to how I spend my time than I did in high school. Yet each semester that I’ve taken a humanities class here, I’ve chosen one that seems productive for my development as a person with worldly knowledge—classes about psychology and Chinese history and language, and this next semester, one about the social problems of nuclear energy—because that’s the only way I can justify taking time away from more important technical pursuits. Like, is me making music going to help us solve climate change? Probably not—at least not as much as working on nuclear fusion will. Knowledge of history, on the other hand? Pretty useful for someone working on nuclear fusion.

It comes down to what I consider important and valuable enough to spend my time on. Science gave me a sense of one-minded purpose, which saved my life but also entrenched in me the mindset that any action not propelling forward my career as a physicist is an action not worth taking.

Math’s the closest I get nowadays to the act of creation, something I do because the convenient excuse of “it’ll make me better at physics” can shroud my real motive, the fact that fitting these pieces of the universe together—like fitting words and scenes together, like colors and silhouettes, these manifolds and holomorphic functions—makes my heart sing.

I want to spend more time on art, writing, music. I crave it, emotionally and physically. What am I afraid of—judgment? Failure? Or the thought that it will slow down my worthy technical pursuits? For now, I settle for flecks of time between my work, when I can feel that I’m biting into that peach and crossing my fingers that the taste will linger.
