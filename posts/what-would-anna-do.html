what would Anna do?

April 27, 2024

When I decided in junior fall that I wouldn’t be applying to graduate school, I felt relieved, even though I didn’t know what I’d do instead.

Little in my life changed. I made motions towards becoming more corporate-pilled by learning C++ and taking a circuits class. Instead of daydreaming about working long nights in grad school, I daydreamed about being unemployed. That was about all.

Two weeks into the spring semester, the image of grad school returned like a specter. Every Tuesday and Thursday, in my sole graduate class, I sat at the back of a little room shaped like Nevada to listen to Wolfgang Ketterle speak about atoms like mysterious yet legible beasts. Tattooed and turtlenecked 23-year-olds populated the neighboring seats. The guy next to me offered me a pineapple cake once in the middle of class, then rummaged through both his jacket pockets and his backpack pouches with increasing desperation before apologizing profusely as “it must be in my other jacket”. Basically, the other students in the class did not seem so different from myself.

Secretly I hoped that, through this class and Junior Lab, I would uncover a latent penchant for experimental physics and realize that AMO was my true calling. I wanted to want to go to grad school—it felt like a path to greatness, a romantic excursion to a land where the only thing you had to think about was how to jiggle your atom correctly, a place to convene with like-minded souls, and most of all, self-evident proof that I deserved to come to MIT in the first place.

In some ways, going to grad school feels like the comfortable option. Of course it’s viewed as the default path forward for a physics major, but even beyond that: pinning a PhD as the goal seems to make my struggling academic career worth it, like, sure I suck at my classes but, see, there’s this research I know I really want to do. And I know how to navigate the dynamics of a research group and the steady thrum of classes, against the familiar backdrop of a college campus. In an office or a startup, I would be starting from scratch.

On the other hand, thinking concretely about grad school admissions for atomic physics makes me cower. This feeling is new, what was missing from my previous ruminations on grad school: the fear of rejection, of having to own up to the fact that I’m just not good enough to do something I care about and love learning about—something I didn’t find in physics until this semester. Somehow, people overcome this fear and apply to grad school anyway.

So how should I go about making the decision? I don’t have any long-term goals to measure up the outcomes of grad school vs not-grad-school against. But thinking about alignment with what I want to see being worked on right now in the world does offer some guidance. I care, with urgency, about biodiversity and renewable energy—I want more people to be working on these things. And going to graduate school for atomic physics doesn’t help with that.

Additionally, I’ve always operated on vibes, recollecting how I felt about individual experiences but never putting my actual desires into words. Solar car was boring; plasma physics research was challenging but not fulfilling; counting salmon is fun but I want a more technical, physics-based challenge.

When I had a conversation with the PI of my salmon counting research, she asked, forcing me for the first time to express a concrete preference: what kind of work do you like to do day-to-day? I flailed around for a bit saying things like well, coding is fun, until something tumbled out of my mouth which felt surprisingly clear and true: I like to build things, to create, whether with circuits or code or words. This desire, again, feels like it would be better satisfied outside of grad school.

At my urging, my other professor—whom I worked with over IAP on a nuclear security UROP—told me about his own journey, which involved writing the Iran nuclear deal before teaching here. I gave ten years of my life to that deal, he said, and it’s just gone now.1 You can’t be sure that any of your work will last, so you should at least try to spend your time around people you respect and want to be like.

That seems like the best indication I can hope for. In the end, when I think of the kind of person I most want to be like, neither the promising young grad student nor the Google engineer making millions come to mind. Instead, I think of the people jumping into lakes with me at my internship last summer, 30-year-olds who knew what they wanted and knew how to get it—like Anna, who fucked off after finishing high school to build pyrotechnics for Burning Man and train under a Shaolin monk and was just now deciding to get a degree, and who was generally worldly and wise and marvelously agentic. I still think about her (What Would Anna Do?) whenever I have to make a difficult decision. And she hasn’t failed me yet.

1

The Iran nuclear agreement was activated in 2014 as a compromise where Iran would cease its enrichment of uranium in return for relief from sanctions. The U.S. withdrew from this deal in 2018.
