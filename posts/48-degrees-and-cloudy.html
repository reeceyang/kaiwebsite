48 degrees & cloudy

May 19, 2023

My girlfriend and I went to Provincetown this week. Finals are next week, and we both had a lot of studying to do, but I’m leaving for LA right after finals and we’ve wanted to visit Cape Cod together for the past year, so we figured there was no better time than right after classes ended.

We stayed in a small, secluded Airbnb, a 15-minute walk from the oceanfront. Our first night there, we put our things down and ate takeout from Kung Fu Dumplings, the closest place that had been open earlier when the ferry landed. The food was so much better than I expected for a place called Kung Fu Dumplings. We wolfed down the lo mein, the custard buns, and yes, the soup dumplings.

I had checked the weather in Provincetown beforehand and saw that the forecast for the next few days was 68 degrees and sunny, so I packed accordingly. Upon arriving in Provincetown, it was actually 48 degrees and cloudy. All the locals seemed perturbed by this as well: we walked past people bundled in winter coats, pulling their hoods over their heads against the wind, grumbling about the sudden change in temperature.

Though our plans to sunbathe on the beach and swim in the ocean were foiled by this development, I felt soothed just staying in a cold, quiet house with only my partner’s typing and the crackle of the Franklin stove to break the silence. The cold made the feeling of a warm blanket draped over my legs more gratifying, made it easier to hold my partner close at night.

I had been avoiding working on an overdue pset for the past few days, but it felt easy to start now. I worked until I was too sleepy to think; we went to bed; I kept working in the morning.

We took a break that next day, around noon, to visit the East End Market and buy groceries. We walked on the side of the road (there was no sidewalk, as is common in Provincetown), stepping off every few minutes to let cars pass. The walk to the market was enveloped in lush greenery: we were surrounded by trees, bushes, bogs. At the market, we bought rice, beans, chickpeas, and some vegetables; on a whim, I bought a hemp-based CBD drink that was strawberry-lavender flavored. Its glass outsides advertised a “calming effect”. It ended up being quite tasty, and indeed after drinking it I felt calm, though it was difficult to tell whether it was due to the drink or the simple fact of being in Provincetown.

How I woke up in Provincetown was different from how I wake up in Boston. In Provincetown, the room fills with soft light as early as five a.m., the birds chatter so loudly that it wakes up me and my partner (a notoriously heavy sleeper). It’s cold outside the blanket, so I resist getting up for a bit; and then I do, and turn on the Franklin stove, and as the house fills with heat I change into my day’s clothes and drink water.

It reminds me of Colorado a bit, where I grew up—how cold and how quiet and how still, waking up because you should rather than fighting alarm after alarm. Resistance to rising; alertness once risen, the boundless energy that you think should come in the morning, washing in naturally.

I like the city, too; I love the view out my window in Boston, and how the room is bathed in an eerie blue-green light if you are up early enough, and the fact that I can get whatever flavor of boba I want at any time of day. But I do miss the peace and comfort of seclusion, icy fingers, no one outside. I am glad it was 48 degrees and cloudy this time.
