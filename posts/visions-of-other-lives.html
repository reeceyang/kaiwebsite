visions of other lives

June 03, 2023

I’m in LA with my family for a couple of weeks before I go to Santa Fe for my summer internship. So far, each day has felt pretty much the same.

I want to test out of MIT’s intro biology course in the fall, so I’m going through the relevant chapters of Campbell right now. I read the chapter first, trying to understand everything; highlight everything I want to make a flashcard out of; and then go through the chapter again to make the flashcards. “Flashcard” is a slightly misleading term because most of these cards are not flash; some of them are quite long, in fact. I create and review these cards using Anki, which is a software I quite enjoy because it requires little to no optimization on my part (e.g., to make cards that are flash). I just write my little definitions and questions and trust that Anki will force me to grind through them enough to cram everything in my brain. Of course I like to write good questions, but the beauty of Anki is that this is not necessary to still absorb the material well.

I also want to take complex analysis in the fall, but a friend who is a math major (and who just graduated!) told me that the class is quite difficult. He recommended I study the textbook Visual Complex Analysis first for a preliminary grasp of the material. I went online and looked up this textbook and found that, for the first time since it was published 25 years ago, a new edition of this textbook was printed in 2023! So after paging through the PDF and becoming sufficiently enamored with it, I bought it on Amazon for the relatively cheap price of $45. So far I have read the first chapter.

Since I failed quantum mechanics this semester, I will be retaking it in the fall. I imagine it will be easier this time around, but I still want to study it over the summer so I’m better prepared. I haven’t yet decided on the best way to do this, though—I felt tired of quantum mechanics at the end of spring and didn’t make the effort to fit it into my immediate study schedule, when there was so much else I was excited about.

Another goal for this summer was to continue working out consistently and become more fit. I can’t tell if I’m making any progress on this front, but I’m trying: I have gone to the apartment gym almost every day for an hour since coming here; yesterday I read a book about the science of strength training at Barnes & Noble; I’m tracking my food intake and trying to increase the amount of protein I eat. I’ll have to change up my routine yet again when I move to Santa Fe, but I plan to keep swimming and lifting.

Apart from these things, I take the dogs to the dog park; I play Minecraft with my girlfriend; I allow myself a steady, shameful intake of fiction; and that is my life. Although I feel happy with how I’m spending my time right now, I have been thinking more about what I really want to do with my life, and, thus, my days. I mean this not in a career-focused, “what work do I want to pursue” way, but in a “what kind of person do I want to be” way.

A few different visions:

I could be someone confident, strong, and daring—spending hours outside in the sun and snow, traveling over mountains and over tides with open palms of callouses and a wide, muscled chest, building a body that bends to my will;

someone dedicated to their work, an intellectual sea, reading Newton and Seneca and becoming a finely tuned instrument, an intense academic who wears trenchcoats and blazers;

someone bursting with creativity, gripping a pen or brush with ferocity, stacks of used canvas and thickened, softened notebooks filling corners of my desk, sketching out sculptures at museums and merging words with color in my spare time;

but can I be all of these—or, the real question, should I try to be all of these? I sought to be an artist once, and then I abandoned this in favor of science. It feels to me like I can only put all of myself into one endeavor at a time. I feel scattered and unfocused otherwise, spread thin over too many pursuits.

I know there are people who accomplish all this simultaneously. But time feels so scarce. How do they do it?
