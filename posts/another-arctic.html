Another Arctic

December 01, 2025 
      I.


      On your cypress desk sat leather-bound
      books, globes dusted, envelopes piled.
      I reached for the photo album; you frowned
      as if omission preserved some wild

      blind passion between us. As if
      you’d stay my passage through a fire
      or prolong the motion: your thumb split
      my ribcage open, heart gouged entire.

      My body first met hard, dry atmosphere
      at your hand’s pull. The sun seared arcs into
      my vision, the tips of glaciers appeared
      (sad ends to those endless cliffs I knew

      from a past life) in glances I stole
      of sea’s face inverted through your porthole.


      II.


      What enchanted me was not only you,
      but the new gods, who rearranged the world
      to let your transatlantic voyage through
      our waters, then-unseen and unpearled.

      When I left you in the morning, I kept
      from singing. It took effort. I had sung
      for years, imagined the sun-facing deck
      of a vessel from whose crested edge hung

      licks of foam, damp netting, pebbled scales,
      chains weighed down by rust and blood, trawling
      the seabed. Springing forth silver tails
      in the thousands. That slice of sun not seen—

      eclipsed by ocean’s end—man’s beginning—
      your power, I thought, unfairly thrilling.


      III.


      The album beckoned to me in your sleep.
      I paged through it and recognized your gaze
      in a sea of men whose labor you’d reap,
      who’d unfurled sails on blustering days,

      drawn beauty from carnage, cities from blubber
      and blubber from dignified creatures. Beside
      your supine body, I touched another
      weightless boy dreaming of unbroken glide

      through metropole on mechanical wings,
      by windows reflecting amber lamplight
      under a sky and stars I’d never seen—
      the known world’s edge graced from that great height—

      while I dreamed of another Arctic,
      a home I’d mourn after we parted.
