things that matter

December 03, 2023

I spent my first and second years of college in a state of constant, high-level confusion. I didn’t know what to do in the future, but more urgently, I didn’t know what I wanted to do with the rest of my college career. This lack of direction plagued every decision I made. In sophomore year, I grew so fed up with physics—my declared major—that I didn’t complete a single physics class, opting instead to throw myself into math and humanities.

To some extent, I learned things and enjoyed myself. But as someone who had always been relentlessly purpose-driven in high school—although this purpose changed over time, from colonizing Mars to improving semiconductor technology to developing nuclear fusion, the skills I wanted to learn were always wrapped up neatly and clearly motivated by my science competitions—the new hole in my ambitions nagged me daily, gave me whiplash like encountering a missing step on a staircase.

I finally took a step back from this during the summer and resolved to just enjoy myself in Santa Fe, knowing that anyway all the months of soul-searching I’d gone through during the school year had amounted to nothing but wasted emotional toil on nights that I should have spent psetting. And somehow in the summer I gained some great sense of clarity without even knowing what I wanted to do in the future, while having no clearer ambition than before.

I think that I’m now able to answer a question that has been increasing in urgency: should I get a PhD?

This seems like the default path for many physics majors at MIT. Maybe 80% of people talk about applying to grad school as though it is a given; the others go into some physics-related industry, become consultants, or work in finance. There are exceptions, but not many—I don’t personally know a single physics major who went into industry instead of grad school/finance/consulting, let alone a different career path altogether.

To me, getting a PhD seems like a great opportunity to dedicate a few years to honing your skills to work on a problem you really want to solve. Although a PhD is often treated as a general prerequisite for more advanced technical work, I don’t think that alone would be a sufficient motivation for me. Instead, I think the ideal situation is something like this: I identify a problem I want to solve, something I care so deeply about that I would dedicate 5-10 years of my life to it; I realize that this requires some level of collaboration and technical skill that I don’t have, and which a PhD program would be better suited to providing than other options would; I apply to PhD programs with advisors who care specifically about this problem area and can help me either solve this problem or solve a more fitting (for whatever reason—feasibility, relevance, etc) problem in the same space.

I have no data as to whether most students applying to grad school share this mindset, and I won’t speculate. I do know that, right now, there are a lot of problems I care deeply about and want to solve, but I’m not convinced that getting a PhD is the best next step to solving any of them. I’ll go more into detail on these problems in a future post, but here’s an example—the U.S. electrical grid sucks in many ways which, in part, restrict adding renewable capacity and the ability for greater electrification. Making it suck less is a really important problem that I would love to work on. It’s something that a bunch of PhDs are working on, but it’s also something that I imagine you could have a lot of impact on if, say, you worked at a company directly building grid infrastructure.

Which path has the potential for greater impact? I don’t know. I’ve spent most of my twenty years on this Earth in school; I really only have a way to gauge the impact of academic work. So I’ve resolved to graduate with a Bachelor’s in physics—maybe a Master’s in nuclear engineering if I’m feeling spicy and want to do the five-year program—and go out into the world and work for a while before maybe circling back to higher education.

Another big reason I came to this conclusion was the people I met during the summer. Previously, the people in my life whom I wanted to learn from most were people who had dedicated their lives to academics: other students who managed their time flawlessly, or worldly, all-knowing professors.

Over the summer I met people who had taken years off to travel the world, who worked jobs ranging from pyrotechnics to Navy submarine nuclear engineers. I thought these people were so cool—so self-knowing, laid-back, empathetic. Those are qualities I want, too; and I think they’re better fostered by taking time off from school to work and travel than by another five years inside university classrooms.

Maybe I’ll change my mind on this in the future, but for now it’s given me some direction for the rest of college: I think majoring in physics and math has given me very good problem-solving skills, and I want to continue to improve those; I also want exposure to different areas of study, want to learn electrical engineering (I’m taking a circuits class this spring!) and history and international affairs. The next few semesters appear to me brimming with potential. There are so many people I want to work with, to find out how their little corners of the world connect to all the other little corners of the world, before I go off and find my little corner as well.
