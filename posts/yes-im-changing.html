yes i'm changing

June 04, 2022

I’m two weeks out from freshman year now, and it feels like it’s been ages since my last classes and exams. If you asked me now, “how’s MIT?” I think the first thing that would come to mind is: “not what I expected.”

MIT hasn’t been disappointing, necessarily. I’ve forged more, and yet shallower, relationships within the span of this year than I imagined I would. My weeks are fuller, and yet less busy, than I thought they would be. East Campus – the dorm where I spent my freshman year – was infinitely richer, and yet a worse fit for me, than I fantasized.

I came into MIT with a lot of preconceived notions about what would make me happy here, and what kind of person I would be here. Post-freshman year me would be power-tool-wielding, solar-car-driving, social, athletic.

But none of those have come to fruition. I moved out of EC after spending a year there. I quit SEVT, MIT’s solar car team. I haven’t laid fingers on a single power tool all year, even though I built things all the time in high school. I’ve made some friendships, but none as deep as I’d hoped. I still don’t have an exercise routine (though I’m working on it!).

Instead, my best experiences thus far at MIT have been totally spontaneous: taking Chinese classes and enjoying them immensely; taking a math class this semester, which ended up being my favorite class; joining Terrascope; rushing tEp; accepting a classmate’s offer to pset, and forging an unexpected friendship from it; being part of the musical theater guild.

I want to explore more this summer and next semester, do more things spontaneously, say yes to more. I’ve spent a very long time saying no to anything I thought wasn’t 100% worth my time, but I’ve found this year that sometimes I’m flat out wrong about what will be fulfilling or what will make me happy. I’m at a place where maybe a little overcommitment, a little dabbling, can help me grow.

Above all, I’ve been humbled. I no longer believe that I’m a good person at heart, nor that I’m necessarily cut out for the work that I want to do. In high school, I thought that things would work out for me no matter what, but now I don’t think that’s true.

People say that MIT breaks you down and then rebuilds you. Certainly, I have been broken down. I hope now that I can be rebuilt – that I can become a kinder and more capable person in my remaining time here.
