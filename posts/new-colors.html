new colors

October 10, 2024

When I went to CVS afterwards to pick up my prescription, I glanced around to see if I should buy anything while I was there. I don’t even remember if this CVS had flowers, but instinctively I thought of getting them, not a bouquet wrapped in sparkly paper but a raw awkward-looking bunch, because she’d loved—ever since we started dating—receiving flowers from me. After she chopped their ends off and arranged them in a vase, those wilted messes I bought from sorority fundraisers invariably perked up and illuminated the room for weeks to come.

I’d been thinking of getting her flowers for a month now and never got around to it. Then, belatedly, after the thought had time to settle pleasantly in my mind, I realized the fantasy of picking out a bunch of artificially-colored lavender roses and running back to her apartment and handing them over with a grin and a hug that had been possible just an hour ago was now totally out of the question.

“It hasn’t been filled yet. Give us a few minutes,” the pharmacy clerk told me. A man sang through the speakers over a tinny guitar, something like I promised I wouldn’t fall in love, but then I found her. I tilted my face up towards the ceiling so the tears would percolate back into my face where they came from and hoped no one would think I was crying because of the ten-minute delay in filling my prescription.

I took a nap on the couch that night holding on to this stuffed lion my roommate Uzay had that you could flip inside out to show either a smiling face or an angry face. It would have felt symbolic except that there was nothing bipolar about the situation as I’d expected, neither a quiet assurance that I’d made the right decision nor the sting of regret. Instead, everything was drenched in an emotion very plain, very straightforward: a single-tipped arrow of grief.

“It’ll all be okay, Kai,” said Uzay. “You’ll see new colors.”

I didn’t know about new colors, but I was certainly feeling new sensations, mainly a persistent dampness. I went into the office at five the next morning and stared with raw, watery eyes at my research code. At first I tried blowing my nose to get rid of the snot but, being continually on the brink of tears, the mucus regenerated faster than the skin of my nostrils. The junction between my sinuses and throat felt cold and wet for the next three days, so long that it began to feel less like I was the one producing the mucus and more like an external agent had injected my nose with saline, like when I’d gone surfing a few weeks ago and seawater dripped out of my nose unbidden for hours after whenever my head rocked into a new angle.

So many things felt so unfamiliar that it kept seeming my circumstances must have been thrust upon me by a looming outside force when actually it had all been my doing. One dizzying fact was that I was no longer able to verbalize my situation, even inside my head. There was no word for what she was to me now: not my girlfriend or ex or friend but rather some chimeric trinity. There was no word for what I was: taken not by a person any longer but by three years of time clutching at me all at once; single though rather than inhabiting one body carrying the expectations of one person, instead I didn’t even fill my body up the whole way, couldn’t feel my toes when my shriveled soul coagulated in my fingers, couldn’t feel my face when I relaxed against the wall and everything pooled into my feet. In some ways I was more half a person than a whole person.

Even after I had stabilized a bit, everything kept feeling new. I haven’t been single for seven years—well before my current concept of self formed. Now there was no one else to crystallize my sense of self through. A sanity check on all my big decisions. Constant reassurance and encouragement. A mirror, proof that I existed in the eyes of other people. Now I just floated through the world, untethered, transparent. New experiences collected in my mind and stayed there, in expectant, stagnant pools of memory. I didn’t know anymore what to do with them.
