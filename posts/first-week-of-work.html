first week of work

June 17, 2023

I started work this past week. I’m an intern in a research program with some other students. They come from all over the US, from all different backgrounds—the oldest person I’ve met so far is 33 years old! So far, my days have gone like this.

I wake up around 6 a.m., eat breakfast, and walk to the gym to work out for an hour. Then I take the bus to work, where usually we have a two-hour lecture in the morning. After that, I do some work on the computer, which so far has consisted of either reading papers or setting up software; I eat lunch in the afternoon; and then I keep working until 5 p.m. and take the bus home. I cook dinner and pack the leftovers for the next day. I have a couple hours free at this point, and I usually use them on reading Campbell and talking with my girlfriend. Then I sleep around 10 p.m.

Repetitive as it sounds, I’ve really been enjoying this routine. I have much more energy throughout the day than I did in Boston; I can’t help but feel happy when I look outside at the endless pine trees and ochre canyons; and I am super interested in the research I’m doing. I’m not sure what caused this change from my depressed spring semester self who was struggling just to stay afloat: less pressure to make deadlines and satisfy my housemates? More freedom to choose what I do outside of work? Greater number of trees per area?

I think being in a place with virtually endless panoramic views of mountains and trees is good for me. On the bus home from work, I pass by the same scenes every day, and every day they really do make me smile and feel happier. I like being in such proximity to untended ground.

And I think being really interested in my work and having a balance of structure and autonomy has also been helpful. (By “structure” I really just mean having people check in on me twice a week and having to be in a physical office for eight hours a day. Any more than that would throw me off the rails.)

My research is on core-collapse supernovae and how these explosions occur. Currently, the dominant explanation is the neutrino-driven mechanism, but the physics behind this is still fuzzy, and it is affected by rotation and magnetism and turbulence—very hard things to simulate when you put them all together! With every additional paper I read, the ratio of knowledge I have about the field to questions I have about the field shrinks and shrinks. But I’m really excited to learn more and test my ideas in simulations and do fun math.

Some interesting notes from the past week:

  • I saw a ten-lined June beetle while waiting for the bus outside the gym! It was large, oval, and pink. It was crossing a stream of ants, but it kept getting ants stuck on its legs, so it would stop to shake the ants off, but then some ants would crawl onto another leg.

  • On my first bus ride to work, we passed a hillside where many prairie dogs were standing up alertly in the morning sun, looking very important.

  • Eight ounces of tempeh contains 51 grams of protein. I was flabbergasted when I saw this information on the tempeh package at Trader Joe’s; I immediately bought the tempeh, and a few days later, I marinated it overnight and pan-glazed it. It was delicious.

  • I turned out to be severely underdressed for work. I show up in T-shirts and basketball shorts; all the grad students dress in ironed button-up shirts and slacks. But T-shirts and basketball shorts are all I brought—I thought Santa Fe would be really hot!

  • As far as I can tell, there are four (4) total women at work, out of about 35 people. This is the worst gender ratio for anything I’ve ever seen except the Battlecode awards dinner (which was one woman to like 40 guys).

  • Some guy called Cowling proved in the 1900s that an axially symmetric magnetic field cannot be produced by dynamo action (where “dynamo action” refers to a bunch of moving, electrically conductive fluid producing a self-sustaining magnetic field). Then Saturn turned out to have a magnetic field dipole oriented only one degree away from its axis of rotation, which was a big fuck-you to Cowling.
