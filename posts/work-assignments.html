work assignments

June 01, 2022

I’m living in tEp for the summer. It’s a co-ed frat across the river from MIT, and living here for the past two weeks has felt very different from living in East Campus, where I was for the last year.

House members at tEp have to complete a work assignment every Sunday. For the past two Sundays, mine has been sweeping the basement corridor and bike room. The first time was relatively painless; the second time, I had just returned from the Boston Calling music festival, where I’d been subjected to a continuous, omnidirectional slurry of secondhand smoke, and I felt pretty nauseous and tired while I swept. But still, I finished my work assignment.

We also have cook and clean shifts. Every Sunday through Thursday night, two people cook dinner for the house, and one person cleans up afterwards. I cooked last night – I made a creamy Italian-ish quinoa soup, and Eden, my cook shift partner, made fries and peach cobbler. People really liked it, which made me happy.

There is some constant responsibility that comes with living at tEp, and things feel solid here in a way that is difficult to encapsulate. The strain in my thighs, lifting furniture up the stairs; the weight of the iron chain that holds open the back door; the ache in my arms stirring a pot of enough soup for twenty.

Before moving here, I was worried about the responsibility inherent in being part of tEp. The functioning of the house as a whole depends so heavily on each individual, especially since there are only around fifteen people living in this massive five-story building. During the school year, I had depressive episodes on a pretty much weekly basis that made it hard just to trek to tEp and cook for two hours, let alone also take up work assignments and hosting social events and everything else that comes with being a member.

But I think actually experiencing life at tEp has had the opposite effect on me. My sleep schedule is better than it’s been all year; I’ve maintained a consistent exercise routine; having responsibility for the place I live in makes it feel like more of a home to me, more of a place that is mine.

I’m also working full-time on my UROP. I use Toggl to track the amount of time I spend working each day. So far, I’ve just been reading relevant papers and working through a basic plasma physics textbook to learn some of the fundamentals. I’m meeting this Friday with Abtin, the grad student who’s my direct supervisor, to clarify some questions on the papers.

I’ve had some doubts over the past year about whether physics research is right for me, so I’m hoping that this summer will either assuage or amplify those doubts. I’ve really enjoyed being able to sink into reading about experiments and electromagnetism and plasma phenomena for hours a day, and I get paid for it. That’s such a crazy deal.

Working eight hours a day is hard, though. Maybe it’s partly because I don’t have an office. The longest amount of time I’ve managed to work in a day thus far is five and a half hours of really good, focused work. I need to get better at focusing for long periods of time and managing my time. This is one of the things that makes me doubt whether I’m cut out for research – a lot of people talk about losing track of time to the point of forgetting to eat or sleep when they’re working on research. And I haven’t experienced that. I worry that I don’t have enough passion for the subject to get through grad school.

But, at the same time, it’s not like I’m really working on research yet. I’m just reading and doing problems right now. I’m excited to actually get started on research, probably sometime next week, and see how I feel about it.

Being a semi-adult is hard. Taking care of not only my own space, but tEp’s space and the house’s food needs as well, and working a full-time job… It has been tiring. But it’s also been very freeing.
